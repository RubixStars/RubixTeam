Edge of the World
A Dungeons & Dragons One-Shot Campaign — Anno Domini 1623
3 Hours 4–6 Players Levels 3–4 Flat Earth Setting Nautical Adventure
Campaign Document — For the Dungeon Master's Eyes
Campaign Overview

The year is 1623. The world is flat — every scholar knows this. Ships that sail too far simply… fall. And yet, no one has ever returned to say so with certainty.

— ⚓ —

The Premise

A group of geography students at the Royal Academy of Cape Colony in the Dutch settlement of Cape Town, South Africa, have become obsessed with a single question: What is truly at the edge of the world? Ancient charts show a great waterfall. Theologians say the void. Sailors speak of sea monsters that guard the rim. The students pool their savings, sell their instruments and books, and raise enough gold to commission a crew of rough pirates who are desperate — or greedy — enough to take the job.

Together, students and pirates sail the Witte Horizon (White Horizon), a newly built three-masted fluyt. The voyage takes them through uncharted waters, past mysterious islands, into a kraken's hunting grounds, through a pirate blockade, and finally to the literal edge of creation itself.

Player Characters

Players choose to be either Geography Students (scholars, navigators, naturalists — light armor, intelligence/wisdom focused) or Pirates (fighters, rogues, barbarians — heavy hitters, sea-experienced). The party should have a mix of both. Provide the following premade character archetypes or let players build their own at Level 3.

Student Archetypes

Cornelius van Dijk — Human Wizard (Cartographer). INT 18, spells include Detect Magic, Identify, Fly. Carries a prized astrolabe.

Beatrix Hartmann — Half-Elf Bard (College of Lore). CHA 16, knows sea shanties that actually buff the crew. Secretly writes everything down for posterity.

Yusuf al-Rashid — Human Cleric (Knowledge Domain). WIS 17, originally from Moroccan trade family. Speaks Arabic, Dutch, and Portuguese. Has a theory the edge is a gateway to paradise.

Pirate Archetypes

Griet "Iron Griet" Bosman — Human Fighter (Battle Master). STR 18, ex-VOC soldier turned freebooter. Short fuse, loyal when paid.

Daan "Slippery Daan" Visser — Human Rogue (Swashbuckler). DEX 18, charming, always looking for an angle. Hides his fear behind jokes.

Naledi of the Cape — Khoikhoi Ranger (Hunter). DEX 17, WIS 15. Local guide hired last minute. Knows the southern sea better than anyone — and suspects the edge is sacred ground.

The Ship: Witte Horizon

DM Note: The ship is its own character. It has 300 HP (treated as a construct), AC 15, and can take damage from attacks. If it reaches 0 HP it sinks — game over. Players can make repairs during rests (DC 14 Carpenter's Tools check restores 2d10+10 HP). Remind players of the ship's condition regularly to build tension.

Dimensions: 90 ft long, 25 ft beam. Three masts. Cannon ports (8 cannons, 3d10 damage each, reload action). Crew quarters below, captain's cabin aft, cargo hold beneath. Built in Cape Town docks — she smells of fresh pine resin and tar.

Pacing Guide (3 Hours)

0:00 – 0:30
Act I — Cape Town: The Gathering & Departure

Introductions, hiring the crew, shopping, and setting sail. One roleplay encounter (dockmaster bribe/persuasion).

0:30 – 2:00
Act II — The Open Sea: Islands & Monsters

Three major encounters: Fog Island (exploration), Pirate Ambush (ship combat), Kraken Attack (boss fight + near-quit moment). Interspersed with short RP moments.

2:00 – 2:40
Act III — The Edge of the World

Arrival, the Guardian Encounter, the Revelation. Emotional climax.

2:40 – 3:00
Epilogue — Return to Cape Town

Quick wrap-up of consequences, awards, and character moments.

Themes & Tone

This campaign blends Age of Sail wonder with cosmic horror and philosophical revelation. The edge of the world is not a trap or a monster — it is a genuine revelation that reshapes the characters' understanding of existence. Play up fear of the unknown, the camaraderie between scholars and rogues, and the bittersweet truth: that knowing doesn't always bring comfort.

Tonally: start grounded and historical (Dutch colonial port, salty sea air, gold and politics), escalate through genuine danger and wonder, and land on something transcendent and slightly terrifying.

Act One
The Departure from Cape Town
⚓ ⚓ ⚓

Setting: Cape Town, South Africa. The Dutch East India Company (VOC) settlement. Year: 1623. A busy harbour of caulked hulls, shouting merchants, gulls and salt wind.

Scene 1 — The Academy Common Room

The scent of ink and tallow candles hangs in the low-beamed room of the Royal Geography Faculty. Maps cover every wall — coastlines of Africa, India, the spice islands. But one wall holds only a great blank expanse, labelled in a student's hand: "HERE BE THE EDGE. UNKNOWN." Cornelius has pinned a note to the centre: "Why unknown? Let us go and see." You have been arguing about this for three months. Today, you have enough money to actually do it.
DM Note: Open with each player describing how their character got involved. Pirates might have been recruited via a tavern notice. Students may have pooled graduation funds. Give each player 30 seconds of spotlight. The key info: they have raised 1,200 gold pieces total (between them). A proper ocean voyage to the edge will cost ~600gp for crew, provisions, and ship rental fees — but as the dockmaster will reveal, no legitimate captain will take this voyage.

Scene 2 — The Docks: Finding a Crew

The harbour of Cape Town reeks of fish oil and ambition. Three-masted VOC ships dwarf the fishing skiffs. A weathered sign above a tavern reads: "THE DROWNED ANCHOR — All Sailors Welcome." Inside: dice games, pipe smoke, and a dozen hard faces that have seen the sea's teeth.

Key NPC: Captain Maarten Hoog (available for hire)

Captain Maarten Hoog
"The edge? [long pause, refills cup] I've heard that question before. Two ships tried last year. The Morgenster. The Vliegende Vis. Neither came back. [leans forward] But neither did they pay me three hundred gold up front. You say you have that kind of money — real coin, not promises — and I'll sail you to the rim of the good Lord's creation itself. My crew don't need to know where we're going until we're too far out to turn back. That's how it works."
Voice: Gravelly, Dutch-accented. Slow and deliberate. Taps finger on table when thinking. Never raises voice — doesn't need to.
Skill Challenge: Negotiating the Crew (Optional RP Encounter)
The players must assemble a full crew of 12. They can persuade/bribe pirates in the tavern. Use the following checks — players need 5 successes before 3 failures:
• Persuasion DC 13 — Convince a reluctant sailor with inspiring speech
• Intimidation DC 14 — Scare off a bully rival trying to poach the crew
• Deception DC 12 — Hide the true destination until they're committed
• Gold bribe (50gp) — Automatic success

Success: Full crew of 12, morale is high (+1 to ship combat checks)
Failure: Only 8 crew, -1 to ship checks and Captain Hoog is grumpy

Scene 3 — Purchasing Supplies

Give players 20 minutes (real time) to roleplay at the docks. Available vendors:

Hendrika's Provisions (General Store)

Salted beef (5gp/week supply for 6), hardtack, fresh water barrels (3gp each), rope (1gp/50ft), lanterns (5sp), healing potions (50gp each — DC 14 Persuasion to negotiate down to 40gp).

Blacksmith Tomás (Weapons)

Cutlasses (15gp), flintlock pistols (30gp, 1d10 piercing, reload action), boarding axes (10gp), chain shirts (50gp), leather armor (10gp).

Old Miriam (Mysterious chart seller)

Old Miriam — the Chart-Seller
"Ah. The edge-seekers. Yes, I can see it in your eyes — that particular madness. [unrolls a salt-stained chart] This shows three things: Fog Island, where the sea forgets itself. The Drowned Shelf, where the kraken sleeps beneath miles of black water. And here — [her finger trembles] — the Cascade Rim. I will sell you this chart for fifty gold. Or you may have it free, if you promise to come back and tell me what you found. I am… very old. I have been waiting a long time for someone brave enough."
Voice: Ancient, almost a whisper. Portuguese accent beneath Dutch. Eyes that are unsettlingly pale — perhaps she has been to the edge before. She will not confirm or deny this.

The chart gives the party advantage on Navigation checks during the voyage.

Scene 4 — Setting Sail

The Witte Horizon creaks as she leaves the dock. Cape Town's white buildings and distant Table Mountain grow smaller. The crew is busy, the rigging sings, and the great blue-grey Atlantic opens ahead of you. Captain Hoog stands at the wheel, pipe unlit in his teeth. He glances back at you once, nods, and faces the horizon. There is no path on the sea. Only direction. South. Into the unknown.
DM Note: Play up the moment of departure. Ask each player: "As the Cape disappears, what does your character do? What are they thinking?" This is the last moment of safety for a long time. Let it breathe.
Act Two
The Voyage — Wonders & Terrors
🌊 🌊 🌊

Encounter 2A — Fog Island (Days 3–4)

On the third morning, a white fog rolls in so thick you cannot see the bow from the stern. The fog smells wrong — like old stone and something sweet, almost floral, utterly out of place on the open ocean. The compass spins. Captain Hoog is cursing under his breath. Then, through the white, the dark shape of an island appears — an island not on any chart except Old Miriam's.
DM Note: This is an exploration encounter, no combat unless players push it. The island exists in a permanent fog bank. It's about 2 miles across, covered in black volcanic rock and strange bioluminescent moss. The goal is to find the Navigation Stone — a lodestone pillar that, when touched, resets compasses and gives the party's navigator advantage on all future navigation checks.

Island Challenges (choose 2–3 based on time)

The Singing Cliffs: DC 13 Wisdom save or be compelled to walk toward them (enchantment). Insight DC 15 reveals it's not magic — it's wind through hollow rock.

The Tide Trap: A canyon fills with seawater every 10 minutes. Players must cross (Athletics DC 12) or find alternate route (Perception DC 14 to spot a rope bridge).

The Island's Guardian — Giant Constrictor Snake: Lives in the ruins of a Portuguese explorer's camp. It is not evil — it is territorial. Players can fight it or use Animal Handling (DC 15) to calm it.

The Navigation Stone: At the island's centre, a 10-foot obsidian pillar humming faintly. Touching it deals 1d6 lightning damage (DC 12 Con save, half on success) and resets all compasses. Also inscribed on it: "Aqui chegámos. Virámos para trás. Deus nos perdoe." (Portuguese: "We came this far. We turned back. God forgive us.")

Captain Hoog — at the inscription
"Portuguese. From the last century, by the weathering. So someone made it this far and… went home. [long pause] Maybe they were wiser than us. [tucks pipe in pocket] Or maybe they just hadn't been paid enough."
Voice: Quieter than usual. This is one of the rare moments Hoog shows vulnerability. Let it linger.

Encounter 2B — The Pirate Ambush (Day 7)

Clear skies, a good wind — and then the crack of a cannon shot across your bow. Two dark ships emerge from behind a rocky promontory, black flags snapping. "THE DAGGER FLEET" is painted in red on the lead ship's hull. A voice bellows through a speaking-trumpet: "Heave to and prepare to be boarded, or we'll sink you where you sail!"
DM Note: This is a SHIP COMBAT encounter. Run it in rounds. Each round, players can: man a cannon (attack roll +5, 3d10 damage), steer (Dexterity/Vehicles DC 13 to avoid next enemy volley), or prepare to repel boarders. The enemy will attempt to grapple and board after 3 rounds. Once boarders are on deck, it becomes standard melee combat. Enemy forces: 12 Pirate Raiders (use Bandit stat block), 1 Pirate Captain Vrouwe Heks (use full stat block in Enemies section).

Ship Combat Rules (Simplified)

Initiative: Roll normally. On each player's turn they choose a ship action AND a personal action.

Enemy Ship HP: 120 HP. AC 14. Each hit destroys rigging/hull. At 60 HP, it lists and slows. At 0, it sinks.

Witte Horizon HP: Track damage. Every 50 HP lost requires a DC 13 Carpenter's check at next rest to repair, or the ship loses a mast and speed drops.

Boarding: If the enemy ship gets within 30 feet and isn't sunk, 1d4+2 pirates swing across on ropes each round. Place them on deck.

Captain Vrouwe Heks — Boarding
"What's this, then? Scholars and landlubbers? [looks at the navigation charts] Oh, you're the fools going to the edge. I've heard of you. You know what happened to the last three ships that tried? [pause] Neither do I. And that's exactly why you should hand over all your gold and go home. This is the last warning the sea will give you."
Voice: Low, dangerous, West African accent. She is not entirely bluffing — she has been further south than most and knows something waits out there. She will retreat if reduced to half HP, leaving a cryptic warning.

Encounter 2C — The Kraken (Days 10–11) [BOSS]

The sea has gone black. Not the darkness of depth — something else, like ink spreading. The water temperature drops sharply. The crew is silent. Then the ship lurches. Something has wrapped itself around the hull below the waterline. A tentacle the thickness of a barrel — no, two — rises from the water alongside the ship, reaching for the rigging. And from below, in the black, two eyes the size of cartwheels open and look directly at you.
DM Note: This is the NEAR-QUIT moment. After round 2 of the kraken fight, have the crew begin to panic. Sailor NPCs scramble for the rowboat. Captain Hoog grips the wheel, white-knuckled, and says nothing. The player characters must choose: fight through, or go home. Make it feel real — the kraken is genuinely terrifying. However it IS beatable. The kraken has a weak point: a barnacled scar on its mantle (DC 16 Perception to notice, deals double damage). Full stat block in Enemies section.

The Near-Quit Moment

Sailor NPC "One-Eye" Pieter — Round 2
"That's IT! I didn't sign up to be EATEN! [runs for the rowboat] The money's not worth it — nothing's worth it — we're going to DIE out here, we're ALL going to—"
Voice: Screaming, terrified. Let him run. Another sailor follows. This should feel like the moment real expeditions collapsed. Players must decide whether to abandon ship, fight on, or something creative.
Captain Hoog — simultaneously, almost to himself
"[gripping the wheel, teeth clenched] My father was a sailor. His father too. The sea has never beaten a Hoog. It is not going to start tonight. [to the players] What do you say? Do we run — or do we find out what's at the edge of everything?"
Voice: Raw, urgent. This is the first time Hoog shows genuine passion. This speech should give players inspiration if they choose to fight on.

Kraken Combat Key Rules

Tentacles on ship: Each tentacle that grabs the hull deals 2d10 damage to the ship per round. Players can attack tentacles directly (each has 30 HP, AC 13).

Ink cloud: Once per encounter, the area becomes heavily obscured for 1 minute. Players within must make DC 14 Con saves or be blinded.

If the Kraken reaches 80 HP damage taken: It retreats into the deep, releasing the ship. The players have won — for now.

Victory moment: The sea clears. The ink disperses. The ship is damaged but sailing. One remaining sailor kneels on deck and begins to cry. Another starts laughing hysterically. This is real. They are real adventurers now.

Naledi of the Cape — After the Kraken
"In my people's stories, the deep water has a guardian. We call it Makulu wa Maji — the Great One of the Water. We do not go into its waters. [looks at the retreating darkness] We just did. [pause] I think this means the edge wants us to find it. Or it is testing whether we deserve to."
Voice: Calm despite everything. She is the most spiritually grounded member of the crew. Her words should land with weight.
Act Three
The Edge of the World
✦ ✦ ✦

Scene 3A — Approach (Day 14)

The sea changes. The waves flatten, as if the water itself is becoming still with anticipation. The horizon ahead is wrong — the line between sea and sky looks brighter than it should, like the sun is rising ahead of you rather than above. The air hums. Not with wind. With something deeper. The crew has stopped talking entirely. Even Captain Hoog holds the wheel with both hands, leaning forward. And then you see it. The sea simply… ends. Perhaps two miles ahead. It ends. It pours off the edge of the world in a waterfall so vast and so loud that you can feel the vibration in your chest from here. The mist that rises from it catches the light and turns everything gold and white and terrifying.

Scene 3B — The Guardian of the Rim

From the mist of the Cascade Rim rises a figure. Not a monster — or not only a monster. It stands fifty feet tall, made of water and golden light and something else, something like starlight held in the shape of a man. Its voice does not come from its mouth. It comes from everywhere, and it speaks in every language at once: "Who approaches the Rim of Creation? Speak your purpose. Those who come to plunder will fall. Those who come to know will be answered."
DM Note: This is a ROLEPLAY encounter, not a combat encounter — unless players attack, in which case use the Rim Guardian stat block. The Guardian asks three questions of the party. Their answers determine what they see at the edge. This is the philosophical heart of the campaign. There are no wrong answers — only honest and dishonest ones. The Guardian detects lies (Insight +12 vs Deception).

The Three Questions

Question 1: "Why did you come here?" (Individual answers from each player.)

Question 2: "What do you fear you will find?"

Question 3: "What will you do with what you learn?"

The Rim Guardian
"[after hearing honest answers] Very well. You are not the first to come here seeking truth. Most who came before came seeking power, or treasure, or proof that their god was correct. They fell. [pause, and the waterfall roars louder for a moment] You may approach. But understand: what is beyond the edge is not nothing. It is everything that was, before your world was spoken into being. You will see it. You will not understand it. And you will carry it with you forever. Are you willing?"
Voice: It is simultaneously all voices — old men, children, men and women and things with no name. Do not try to "do an accent." Speak slowly, in a measured register, with long pauses. Let the silence work.

Scene 3C — The Revelation

The ship drifts to the edge. The waterfall is deafening — the greatest sound you have ever heard, the sound of all the water in the world falling forever into something beneath existence. You look over the side. And beyond the edge of the world, there is… [pause for effect] …light. Pure, sourceless, endless light that is also darkness, that is also colour, that is also sound, that is the feeling of being held and the feeling of falling and the feeling of being exactly where you were always meant to be. It is the face of creation. It is the space where God breathed the world into being. It is the abyss and it is home. And you understand, in a way that will never fully fit into words, that the world does not end here. It begins here.
DM Note: Go around the table. Ask each player: "What does your character see, specifically, in the light?" Their answer should reflect their character's deepest wish or fear. The cartographer sees every map he will ever make, already drawn. The pirate sees her dead crewmates, alive and at peace. The cleric sees the face of God — and it is kind. Give each player their own private moment. This is what they came for.

After the Revelation — The Return

Beatrix Hartmann — writing in her journal, voice breaking
"I have been to the edge of the world. I have seen what is there. And I find I cannot write it down. For the first time in my life, I have no words. [looks up] And I think… I think that might be the point."
Voice: Quiet wonder. Not sad. Not triumphant. Changed.
Captain Hoog — turning the ship around
"Right. [clears throat] I've seen enough. More than enough. [quietly] I think I'll retire when we get back. Buy a plot of land. Far from the sea. [looks back at the waterfall one last time] Or perhaps not. [turns to face forward] All hands! North-northwest! We're going home!"
Voice: Returning to his gruff captain's voice — but it's an act now and everyone knows it. The crack in it is real.

Scene 3D — The Return Voyage & Epilogue

The return voyage is peaceful — the sea parts for them, the winds are favourable, and the kraken does not appear. This is a gift from the Guardian. The journey home takes 10 days.

As Cape Town's harbour comes into view, ask each player to deliver one final line — what their character says (or doesn't say) as they see their home again. Let it be whatever it needs to be.

Table Mountain rises above Cape Town, its famous tablecloth of cloud spread across the summit. The harbour smells of fish and rope and ordinary life. Old Miriam is standing on the dock, as if she knew you were coming. She doesn't ask what you found. She simply looks at your faces — all of your faces, students and pirates alike — and nods once, slowly. And she smiles.
Old Miriam — Final Line
"[very quietly, almost to herself] So it is still there. Good. [looks at Beatrix's blank journal pages] Don't worry, child. Some things do not need to be written. They only need to be carried. [turns and walks away into the crowd]"
Voice: The oldest voice you can manage. Peaceful. Like someone who has been waiting centuries for confirmation of something she already knew.
Non-Player Characters
NPC Compendium & Dialogue Scripts
— 👤 —

Captain Maarten Hoog

Human male, ~50. Grizzled, thick-bearded, VOC veteran turned freelance captain. Wears a battered naval coat. One gold tooth. Commanding but not unkind.

Role: Ship captain. Primary NPC ally throughout. His arc: mercenary indifference → genuine awe → transformation.

Voice: Gravelly Dutch accent. Speaks slowly, means every word. Never uses two words where one will do. Taps finger on surfaces when thinking.

On being hired
"Three hundred gold. Half now, half when we get back. If we don't get back — well, then it won't matter, will it? I've sailed worse waters for less money. Get your gear on my ship by dawn."
Day 5 — to a nervous student
"You're scared? Good. A man who isn't scared of the sea is either a fool or a liar. The sea doesn't care which you are. It'll drown you just the same. Fear keeps you smart. [pause] Just don't let it keep you still."
After the Kraken
"I'm going to need a longer drink than this bottle holds. [looks at the damage] She held. I didn't think she'd hold. [quietly] Good ship. Good damn ship."
At the Edge
"[long silence] My wife told me I'd die at sea. I always thought she meant this kind — water in the lungs, screaming and cold. [pause] I think she meant this kind. Quiet. Certain. Changed forever. [wipes eye roughly] Don't tell anyone I said that."

Old Miriam

Elderly woman of indeterminate origin. Keeps a map stall at the docks. Her eyes are the colour of sea ice. She has been in Cape Town for as long as anyone can remember.

Mystery: She has been to the edge herself, long ago. She will never confirm this, but careful players (Insight DC 16) may guess. She is not magical — she is simply very, very old and very, very patient.

Voice: Barely above a whisper. Portuguese accent beneath Dutch. Takes long pauses between sentences, as if each word is carefully chosen from a great store of words.

Selling the chart
"Fog Island forgets you. The Drowned Shelf remembers everything. The Cascade Rim — [she stops] — the Rim is the end of forgetting and remembering both. Keep this chart dry. Water damages the ink, but the truth underneath cannot be damaged. Go. And come back."
If asked "Have you been there?"
"[very long pause] I have been many places. [returns to sorting charts] The price is fifty gold. Or a promise. Which do you prefer?"

Captain Vrouwe Heks (Pirate Villain)

West African woman, ~35. Leader of the Dagger Fleet, two vessels strong. Escaped from a Portuguese slaving vessel at 17. Has sailed the southern ocean for fifteen years. Fears nothing visible.

Motivation: She's not purely villainous — she doesn't want anyone to reach the edge because she believes it will destroy the world. She has seen something in the deep south that frightened her enough to become its self-appointed guardian.

Voice: Low, clipped, Yoruba-influenced English/Dutch. Does not shout — she articulates. Every word arrives like a thrown knife.

Initial hail
"No one sails south of the fortieth parallel. That's the rule. My rule. The sea's rule. You have ten seconds to turn your ship around before I put a cannon shot through your waterline. I am not asking."
During boarding
"I've sunk four ships trying to reach the edge. I did not enjoy it. I will sink yours too, if I must. I am asking you: why is this worth dying for? What do you think is out there that is worth all of this?"
If reduced to half HP / retreating
"[backing toward her ship] You'll reach it. I can see that now. You're the type. Fine. But when you look over the edge — remember that you chose this. Remember that I warned you. And when you come back changed — and you will come back changed — remember: some things can be unlearned. The edge cannot. [she jumps back to her ship]"

The Rim Guardian

Not a creature. Not a god. Something between. A voice and a shape that the world uses to ask its own questions.

Voice instructions: Speak in a measured monotone. Pause three full seconds between sentences. Do not do an accent. Do not raise or lower pitch. The weight comes from the pace and the words themselves, not performance.

The Three Questions
"Why did you come here? [wait for answers] What do you fear you will find? [wait] What will you do with what you learn? [wait — and then:] You have answered honestly. This is rarer than you might imagine. You may see."
If attacked
"[no change in tone] You choose to fight what you do not understand. This is also common. [combat begins] I do not enjoy this. Come back when you are ready to ask instead of strike."
Farewell
"You have seen. Now go. Live. The question was never what is at the edge. The question was always: who are you, to ask it? You have answered. The edge will remember you. [fading] Go home. Be kind. The world is small and brief and worth every moment of it."

Siege Monster. The kraken deals double damage to objects and structures (including the ship).

Weak Point — Barnacled Scar. DC 16 Perception to notice. Attacks targeting the scar deal double damage. The scar is on the mantle, 20 ft. above waterline — requires a climb check (DC 12 Athletics) or ranged attack at -2 to reach.

Psychic Terror. Any creature that starts its turn within 60 ft. of the kraken must make a DC 17 Wisdom save or be Frightened until the start of their next turn.

Actions (Multiattack)

The kraken makes three attacks: up to two Tentacle attacks and one Bite, or uses Fling.

Tentacle. Melee Weapon Attack: +17 to hit, reach 30 ft. Hit: 20 (3d6+10) bludgeoning damage and the target is grappled (escape DC 18). The kraken can grapple up to 10 creatures simultaneously.

Fling. One Large or smaller creature grappled by the kraken is thrown up to 60 ft. and takes 3d6 bludgeoning damage per 10 ft. traveled. If it hits the ship, the ship takes the same damage.

Bite. Melee Weapon Attack: +17 to hit, reach 5 ft. Hit: 23 (3d8+10) piercing. If the target is grappled, it is swallowed on a hit if it is Medium or smaller (blinded, restrained, 21 (6d6) acid damage per turn — can claw out with DC 20 Str check dealing 30 slashing to the kraken's interior).

Bonus Actions

Ink Cloud (Recharge 5–6). A 60-ft. radius sphere of black ink fills the water and air. Area is heavily obscured. Duration: 1 minute or until dispersed by wind. Creatures entering for the first time on a turn make DC 14 Con save or are blinded.

Hull Crush. The kraken crushes a grappled ship segment. Ship takes 4d10+10 damage. If ship is at half HP, a mast breaks (DC 15 Athletics to save any PC standing on it).

Legendary Actions (3/round, end of another creature's turn)

Tentacle Attack (1 action). One tentacle attack.

Lightning Surge (2 actions). The kraken releases a 5-ft.-wide, 120-ft. line of lightning. DC 16 Dex save, 4d8 lightning damage (half on save).

Rise (3 actions). The kraken rises entirely above water this round — every creature within 30 ft. on the ship makes DC 18 Str save or is knocked prone. The ship takes 2d10 damage from the wake surge.

DM Tip: The kraken retreats once it has taken 80 total HP of damage (roughly 3–4 rounds of focused fire). Don't let the players kill it — they are NOT supposed to be able to. The retreat is not a failure, it's a victory. The sea goes still, the black ink disperses, and the ship is bloodied but intact. Celebrate this moment. They earned it.
