❓ Viva Questions — Snellen's Chart
VivaWhy is the Snellen chart placed at exactly 6 metres?▾

At 6 metres, light rays from an object are virtually parallel (divergence is negligible — less than 0.17 dioptres). This simulates optical infinity, meaning the eye requires minimal to zero accommodation to focus on the chart. The test therefore measures the eye's refractive state without the confounding effect of accommodation.

Practically: distances shorter than 6m would require accommodation → myopes would appear to see better than they actually do.

VivaWhat does 6/18 mean? What about 6/60? CF at 1m?▾

6/18: Patient stands at 6m and reads the line that a person with normal vision can read from 18m — i.e. vision is reduced to 1/3rd of normal.

6/60: Patient reads only the top letter at 6m; a normal eye sees this from 60m. Vision is 1/10th of normal.

CF at 1m: Patient cannot read any Snellen letters even at 1m. Next step is to hold up fingers — if they count correctly at 1 metre, record as "CF at 1m." Below CF: hand movement (HM), then perception of light (PL) ± projection, then no perception of light (NPL = total blindness).

VivaWhat is the pinhole test? What does improvement with pinhole tell you?▾

A pinhole disc (1–1.5mm aperture) is held in front of the eye. It selects only the central paraxial rays, effectively eliminating the effect of refractive error on image quality — any defocused peripheral rays that would otherwise degrade the image are blocked.

Improvement with pinhole: Vision improves → the cause of reduced vision is a refractive error (myopia, hypermetropia, astigmatism, irregular astigmatism). The eye can be corrected with glasses/lenses.

No improvement: Vision stays the same or worsens → the cause is in the media, retina, or optic nerve (cataract, corneal scar, macular degeneration, optic atrophy). Glasses will not help.

Slight improvement: May be partial refractive + partial media/retinal cause.

VivaHow do you test vision in an illiterate patient or a child who doesn't know letters?▾

Options:

1. E-chart (Illiterate E / Tumbling E): Patient indicates direction the arms of the "E" point (up, down, left, right) using hand gesture. No literacy required. Same 6m distance, same notation.

2. Landolt C (broken ring): Patient indicates where the gap in the ring is. Used as international standard.

3. Picture chart (for children): Symbols like house, boat, bird, apple — child names or matches the symbol. Placed at 6m.

4. Kay pictures / Cardiff acuity cards: Specific paediatric VA testing cards with preferential looking.

5. Cardiff Acuity Test / Teller Acuity Cards: Preferential looking — infant looks toward grating; spatial frequency at which preference disappears = VA.

6. VEP (Visual Evoked Potential): Objective measure of vision in infants and non-cooperative children.

VivaWhat is BCVA? How does it differ from presenting VA?▾

Presenting VA (PVA): Vision as the patient presents — with their current glasses if any, or without glasses. This is what the patient actually sees in daily life.

BCVA (Best Corrected Visual Acuity): The best vision achievable after optimal refraction correction with spectacles or contact lenses. Represents the best the visual system can do once refractive error is corrected.

Clinical significance: If PVA is 6/24 but BCVA is 6/6 → the entire deficit is refractive → glasses solve the problem completely. If BCVA is 6/24 → there is an organic cause (media opacity, retinal/optic nerve disease) limiting vision beyond the refractive component.

Medicolegally: Disability/blindness is assessed on BCVA, not PVA. WHO blindness criterion = BCVA <3/60 in better eye.

VivaHow do you record vision if the patient can't even see hand movements?▾

In descending order of vision:

1. 6/6 (or better) — normal
2. 6/X — Snellen notation at 6m
3. CF at Xm — count fingers at specified distance
4. HM positive — can detect hand movement (no specific distance needed, usually 0.5–1m)
5. PL+PR — perceives light AND can accurately project direction (temporal, nasal, superior, inferior quadrants)
6. PL-PR (inaccurate projection) — perceives light but cannot correctly identify direction
7. NPL — no perception of light = total blindness

Why projection matters: PL+PR means the peripheral retina is functioning in the correct quadrant — surgical prognosis is better. PL-PR suggests widespread retinal dysfunction despite some light perception.

❓ Viva Questions — Jaeger's / Near Vision
VivaWhat is the difference between Jaeger's chart and N-notation?▾

Both test near vision but use different print size systems:

Jaeger chart: Uses J-notation (J1 to J7+). Developed by Eduard Jaeger (Vienna, 1854). Print sizes graded by typographic point sizes. J1 = finest (normal near vision). Inconsistent standardisation across manufacturers — J numbers may vary.

N-notation (British Standard): Uses point size of typeface directly (N5, N6, N8, N10...). N6 is standard reading print (equivalent to J1). More reproducible than Jaeger.

Normal near vision: J1 (= N5–N6) at 30–35 cm without glasses in people under 40.

Testing distance: 30–35 cm — arm's length. Held perpendicular to line of sight in diffuse illumination.

VivaWhat is presbyopia? What is the mechanism? How is it treated?▾

Presbyopia: Age-related loss of accommodation resulting in inability to focus on near objects. Typically symptomatic after age 40 (in Indian population, sometimes earlier — 38 years).

Mechanism: The crystalline lens continuously adds new fibres from the epithelium (like an onion) → central fibres become compacted, lose water, increase refractive index, and most importantly lose elasticity. The ciliary muscle contracts normally but the sclerosed lens cannot change shape (accommodative response lost). Helmholtz theory: ciliary muscle contracts → zonules relax → soft elastic lens bulges → accommodation. In presbyopia the lens is too rigid to bulge.

Symptoms: Difficulty reading fine print, need to hold reading material further away, eye strain/headache after near work, better near vision in bright light (pupil constriction → increased depth of focus).

Treatment:
— Reading glasses: convex (plus) lenses. Power = working distance correction (e.g. +1.0D at age 40, increasing ~+0.25D every 2 years to ~+2.50–3.00D by age 55–60)
— Bifocals (distance + reading segments)
— Progressive addition lenses (PALs) — no visible segment line
— Multifocal contact lenses / monovision
— Surgical: conductive keratoplasty (CK), monovision LASIK, accommodating IOL, multifocal IOL, corneal inlays (Kamra, Raindrop)

VivaHow does near vision testing differ from distance testing — what accommodation-related factors must you consider?▾

Key differences:

1. Accommodation is active at near — the lens must increase power. This means near testing includes both refractive status AND accommodative ability.

2. Pupil constricts during near (miosis) — increases depth of focus → patients may read better at near than distance would suggest, even with some refractive error.

3. Convergence occurs simultaneously — binocular near testing checks fusional reserve. Any convergence insufficiency affects near comfort even if monocular near vision is normal.

4. Distance for testing matters: 30–35cm is the standard working distance (arm's length). If a patient has to hold the chart at 50cm to read J1 → they are hypermetropic or presbyopic (reduced accommodation).

5. Near addition: When prescribing, the near addition = the accommodative demand that the ciliary muscle can no longer meet alone. Assess with trial frame at working distance.

❓ Viva Questions — Ishihara Chart
VivaWhat exactly does the Ishihara chart test? What does it NOT test?▾

Tests: Red-green colour deficiency (protan = red defect, deutan = green defect). The plates use pseudo-isochromatic design — numerals are made of dots that differ from the background only in hue (same luminance), so colour-deficient individuals cannot distinguish them.

Does NOT test:
— Blue-yellow colour deficiency (tritanopia) — not detected by Ishihara
— Degree/severity of deficiency (only screening, not quantitative)
— Acquired vs congenital colour deficiency (cannot distinguish)
— Central vs peripheral colour vision independently

For full assessment: Farnsworth-Munsell 100 Hue Test (quantitative, all colour axes), Farnsworth D-15 (quick, severity), Nagel anomaloscope (gold standard for protan/deutan differentiation), City University Test (blue-yellow included).

VivaWhat is the inheritance of colour blindness? Why is it more common in males?▾

Red-green colour blindness = X-linked recessive (OPN1LW gene for protan, OPN1MW for deutan — both on X chromosome at Xq28).

Males (XY): Only one X chromosome — if the defective gene is on that X → colour blind (hemizygous). Prevalence: ~8% of males.

Females (XX): Two X chromosomes — need defective gene on BOTH X chromosomes (homozygous) to manifest. One normal X protects → carriers only. Prevalence: ~0.5% of females. Carrier females have 50% chance of passing defective X to sons.

Tritanopia (blue-yellow) = autosomal dominant (OPN1SW gene, chromosome 7) — equally affects males and females, very rare (0.008%).

Acquired colour deficiency: Can occur with optic nerve disease (red-green defect — like optic neuritis), macular disease (blue-yellow defect), glaucoma (blue-yellow early), toxic optic neuropathy. NOT X-linked.

VivaHow do you perform the Ishihara test correctly? Name sources of error.▾

Correct technique:
1. Diffuse natural daylight or standardised D65 illuminant (colour temperature 6500K) — NOT tungsten, LED, or fluorescent which alter perceived hue
2. Distance: 75 cm from eye, book perpendicular to line of sight
3. Time: each plate shown for 3–5 seconds only (longer = guessing)
4. Test each eye separately with the other occluded
5. Start with plate 1 (control — everyone reads "12" regardless of colour vision)
6. Record responses for each plate
7. Score: ≥17/21 correct = normal; <13/21 = definite colour deficiency

Sources of error (false positives/negatives):
— Incorrect lighting (tungsten → plates appear different hue) → false positives
— Time too long → patient guesses correctly → false negatives
— Dirty/faded plates (colour changes with age/UV) → false positives
— Testing binocularly → misses unilateral acquired defects
— Patient literacy issues (number recognition) → use Colour Vision Testing Made Easy (CVTME) symbols for children
— Very low visual acuity → cannot resolve dots regardless of colour → false positive
— Malingering → normal person claims not to see numbers

VivaA patient sees all plates as "12" on the Ishihara. What could this mean?▾

Plate 1 shows "12" which everyone (normal and colour blind) should read — it is the control/demonstration plate.
However, several subsequent plates show:
— A number visible to normals but NOT to colour-deficient (vanishing plates)
— A DIFFERENT number to colour-deficient than to normals (transformation plates)
— A number visible only to colour-deficient but NOT to normals (hidden digit plates)

If a patient reads "12" on ALL plates (including transformation and hidden digit plates):

1. They may be giving responses without looking carefully (not engaged)
2. They may have very low visual acuity and are guessing the first number they saw
3. Malingering — repeating the same answer
4. Very rarely — some severe colour vision defect responds differently

Action: Repeat carefully with clear instructions; check acuity first; time each plate strictly at 3–5 seconds; use alternate colour vision test.

❓ Viva Questions — Pupil Examination
VivaDescribe the complete light reflex pathway — afferent and efferent.▾

Afferent limb (CN2):
Light → retinal photoreceptors (rods + cones) → bipolar cells → retinal ganglion cells → optic nerve → optic chiasma (nasal fibres from each eye cross) → optic tracts → NOT lateral geniculate nucleus → pretectal nuclei (dorsal midbrain, at level of superior colliculus) → BILATERAL projection to both Edinger-Westphal nuclei

Efferent limb (CN3 parasympathetic):
Edinger-Westphal (EW) nucleus → preganglionic parasympathetic fibres travel in CN3 (outer, superficial position — important!) → ciliary ganglion in orbit → short ciliary nerves → sphincter pupillae → miosis (constriction)

Why bilateral: Pretectal nucleus projects to both EW nuclei → light in one eye causes constriction in BOTH eyes simultaneously (direct + consensual reflexes).

Near reflex arc (separate pathway): Cortex (visual + frontal) → superior colliculus → EW nucleus → ciliary ganglion → sphincter pupillae + ciliary muscle + medial recti (triad: miosis + accommodation + convergence). This pathway is NOT through pretectal nucleus — explains Argyll Robertson pupil (pretectal lesion: light-near dissociation).

VivaHow do you perform the swinging flashlight test? What is a RAPD?▾

Swinging flashlight test technique:
1. Dim the room (semi-darkness — pupils partially dilated for better observation)
2. Ask patient to fix on a distant target (prevents near reflex miosis interfering)
3. Shine bright torch tangentially from temporal side into one eye → observe direct constriction
4. After 2–3 seconds, swing light to the other eye → observe immediate response
5. Normal: both pupils remain equal and constricted
6. Swing back and forth several times, observing carefully

RAPD (Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect) = Marcus Gunn Pupil:
When light swings to the affected eye → instead of maintaining constriction, the pupil dilates (or dilates then sluggishly reconstricts). This is because the signal from the affected eye's optic nerve is weaker than from the normal eye → when compared, the affected eye's input is relatively less → net parasympathetic output decreases → pupil dilates.

Causes of RAPD: Optic neuritis, severe glaucoma (advanced), optic nerve compression, ischaemic optic neuropathy, severe retinal disease (large RD, CRAO, CRVO). NOT found in bilateral symmetric disease (both inputs equally reduced).

Grading RAPD: +1 (brief dilation before reconstriction) to +4 (immediate sustained dilation) using neutral density filters for precise grading.

VivaWhy should you NEVER dilate the pupil in a head injury patient?▾

The pupil is the most important clinical indicator of brainstem/midbrain status in head injury.

Significance of pupil changes in head injury:
— Unilateral dilated fixed pupil → ipsilateral CN3 compression → uncal (transtentorial) herniation — neurosurgical emergency
— Bilateral fixed dilated pupils → brainstem herniation / midbrain compression → very poor prognosis
— Constricted pupils (pinpoint) → bilateral pontine lesion or opioid toxicity
— Asymmetric pupils → localising sign of expanding intracranial haematoma (extradural/subdural)

If you dilate the pupils with mydriatic drops:
— The pupil is pharmacologically fixed and dilated for 6–24 hours
— Any subsequent change in pupil size (the most important monitor of neurological deterioration) will be uninterpretable
— If herniation occurs, the dilated pupils cannot be used to track it
— Neurosurgeons rely on serial pupil checks — mydriasis destroys this monitoring

Also never dilate in: Organophosphate poisoning (pupil constriction is a monitoring parameter for cholinergic toxidrome severity); narrow-angle glaucoma risk; before neurological assessment is complete.

VivaWhat is Horner's syndrome? How do you test for it?▾

Horner's syndrome: Interruption of the oculosympathetic pathway → classic triad of:
1. Ptosis (partial, 1–2mm — Muller's muscle denervation; also lower lid elevation = "reverse ptosis" / Upside-down ptosis)
2. Miosis (small pupil — dilator pupillae denervation; anisocoria greater in dim light)
3. Anhidrosis (decreased sweating — ipsilateral face, if lesion is pre-ganglionic; absent if post-ganglionic after SCG)
+ Enophthalmos (apparent — due to ptosis + elevation of lower lid narrowing palpebral fissure)

Pharmacological tests:
Cocaine 4–10%: Blocks reuptake of noradrenaline → normal eye dilates, Horner's eye does NOT dilate (confirms Horner's; cannot localise)
Apraclonidine 0.5–1% (reverse test): Alpha-agonist with weak alpha-1 → normal eye no change; Horner's eye dilates (denervation supersensitivity). Safer, available alternative to cocaine.
Hydroxyamphetamine 1% (Paredrine — localises): Releases stored NE from post-ganglionic terminals → Pre-ganglionic lesion: dilates normally (post-ganglionic intact); Post-ganglionic lesion: does NOT dilate (terminal depleted)

Causes by level:
1st neuron (central): Stroke, demyelination, lateral medullary syndrome (Wallenberg)
2nd neuron (pre-ganglionic): Pancoast tumour (apex lung), thyroid carcinoma, cervical rib, post-carotid surgery
3rd neuron (post-ganglionic): Carotid artery dissection (neck pain + Horner's = emergency), cavernous sinus, cluster headache

VivaWhat is Argyll Robertson pupil? What is the lesion and classic disease?▾

Argyll Robertson (AR) pupil: Classic finding in neurosyphilis (Treponema pallidum infection of CNS).

Features:
— Bilateral (usually), asymmetric
— Small (miotic) pupils
— Irregular shape
— Light-near dissociation: Does NOT react to light; DOES react to near (accommodation-convergence reflex)
— Poorly dilates with mydriatics (atropine resistance — chronic miosis)

Lesion: Pretectal region of dorsal midbrain. The light reflex afferent fibres synapse here; the near reflex pathway bypasses this region (goes via cortex → superior colliculus). Syphilitic lesion preferentially destroys the light-reflex interneurons in the pretectal nucleus while sparing the near-reflex pathway.

Mnemonic: "AR pupil — Accommodates but doesn't React (to light)." Also called "Prostitute's pupil" — accommodates but doesn't react.

Other causes of light-near dissociation: DM (autonomic neuropathy), Parinaud's syndrome (dorsal midbrain compression — also upgaze palsy + convergence-retraction nystagmus + lid retraction = Collier's sign), Adie's (tonic) pupil (ciliary ganglion lesion — near reaction is slow and tonic, not absent).

Foreign Body Removal

Corneal + Conjunctival FB · Practical Skill
Conjunctival FB SKILL
  1. Check VA before any procedure
  2. Instil topical anaesthetic (0.5% proparacaine or 4% xylocaine)
  3. Evert upper lid (most FBs hide under upper lid!)
  4. Locate FB under slit lamp or loupe
  5. Remove with moistened cotton bud or forceps
  6. Double evert lid for deeper fornical FB
  7. Instil antibiotic drops
  8. Review in 24 hours
Corneal FB SKILL
  1. Check VA; document size/location of FB
  2. Instil topical anaesthetic
  3. Position at slit lamp
  4. Use 26G needle or FB spud
  5. Approach tangentially from side
  6. Remove from edge → center direction
  7. If rust ring: remove with Alger brush (after 24–48h)
  8. Instil antibiotic drops; pad eye if needed
  9. Review in 24h
⚠ Watch Out For
  • • Intraocular FB — X-ray/CT before removal
  • • Perforating injury — don't attempt removal in clinic
  • • Never probe a lacerated or penetrated eye
  • • Vegetative material → watch for fungal ulcer
  • • Iron/copper FB → siderosis/chalcosis if left long
💡 Tips
  • ✓ Always evert upper lid — 80% FBs hide there
  • ✓ Fluorescein stain to see epithelial defect
  • ✓ Rust ring = delayed removal easier
  • ✓ Vegetative FB → give prophylactic antifungal
Eyelid Eversion (Double eversion): Desmarres retractor + muscle hook used when lid can't be everted normally (edema, mass, malignancy etc.)
❓ Viva Questions — Foreign Body Removal
VivaWhat is the first thing you do before removing a corneal foreign body?▾

Always check and record visual acuity FIRST — before any procedure, including instilling anaesthetic. This is mandatory for:

1. Medicolegal documentation: If there is a pre-existing VA deficit from the injury, it must be on record before you touched the patient

2. Clinical assessment: Severely reduced VA before a seemingly superficial FB may indicate deeper penetration or concurrent injury (vitreous haemorrhage, lens injury, retinal trauma)

3. Baseline for monitoring: Compare post-removal VA to confirm improvement

Then: Do the Seidel test (fluorescein stain — look for streaming aqueous = perforation). If Seidel positive → DO NOT remove → refer to ophthalmology emergency → no pressure on eye → apply shield.

VivaWhy do you approach a corneal FB tangentially and not perpendicular?▾

Tangential approach: Needle/spud approaches from the side, parallel to the corneal surface, or at a very shallow angle (10–15°).

Reason:
1. The cornea is only 0.5–0.6mm thick centrally (0.7mm peripherally). A perpendicular approach risks pushing the needle straight through into the anterior chamber → perforation → anterior chamber collapse → iris/lens damage.
2. Tangential approach allows the FB to be scooped out or flicked from the side with a lifting motion → less force directed inward
3. Allows better visualisation of depth at slit lamp
4. Reduces risk of extending any existing epithelial defect deeper into stroma

Direction of removal: From the edge of the FB toward the centre (not from centre outward — risk of pushing debris deeper).

VivaWhat is a rust ring? How long does it take to form and how do you remove it?▾

Rust ring: A brownish ring of iron oxide deposits around the site of a metallic (iron/steel) FB in the cornea. The FB reacts with the oxygen and water in corneal tissues → iron oxidises to iron oxide (rust) → deposits in epithelial cells and superficial keratocytes surrounding the FB.

Time to form: Begins within 2–4 hours of metallic FB contact; well-established rust ring by 24–48 hours.

Why it must be removed: Iron oxide is toxic to keratocytes → ongoing chemical inflammation → delayed epithelial healing → corneal scarring if left. Even after the FB is removed, the rust ring continues to cause toxicity.

Removal technique:
— Ideally delay removal by 24–48 hours after FB removal → rust ring softens → easier to remove cleanly
— Alger brush (battery-operated rotary burr) under topical anaesthesia at slit lamp — most effective
— Alternatively: 26G needle gentle tangential scraping
— Not necessary to remove ALL rust in one sitting — superficial layer first, remainder in 24–48h review
— Deep rust near Bowman's → leave some residual rather than risk scarring (residual rust will migrate outward with epithelial turnover)

VivaA patient comes with vegetative material (paddy husk) in the eye. What special precautions do you take?▾

Vegetative material (paddy husk, plant matter, soil) carries a high risk of fungal contamination → Aspergillus, Fusarium species → fungal keratitis if not properly managed.

Precautions:
1. Remove the FB as usual (with topical anaesthetic, tangential approach)
2. Prophylactic antifungal drops: Start Natamycin 5% eye drops even before any fungal infection is visible — corneal blindness from fungal ulcer is the major risk in India (agricultural workers, commonest cause of corneal blindness)
3. Do NOT use steroid drops — steroids promote fungal growth
4. Close follow-up: Review at 24–48h to look for early signs of fungal ulcer (feathery margins, dry-looking infiltrate, satellite lesions)
5. Culture the FB material if possible — KOH mount from the removed material
6. Educate patient: any increase in pain, redness, or white spot → return immediately
7. Consider adding broad-spectrum antibiotic as well (chloramphenicol or ciprofloxacin) for bacterial prophylaxis

Why farmers are high-risk: Corneal blindness in India is predominantly from fungal corneal ulcers secondary to agricultural (vegetative) trauma — corneal blindness is the main preventable cause of blindness in India.

VivaHow do you evert the upper eyelid? Why is it important?▾

Simple eversion technique:
1. Ask patient to look downward (relaxes LPS → lid more easily everted)
2. Grasp lashes gently between thumb and forefinger
3. Place applicator stick (or Desmarres retractor) horizontally at upper border of tarsus (~8mm above lid margin)
4. Gently pull lashes downward and forward simultaneously
5. Fold lid upward over the stick by rotating your wrist
6. Hold lashes to brow to maintain eversion
7. Examine tarsal conjunctival surface and superior fornix under loupe/slit lamp

Double eversion (for superior fornix): After simple eversion → place Desmarres retractor at superior edge of everted lid → gently depress and roll further → exposes superior fornix completely. Required when FB suspected deeper in fornix or when simple eversion does not reveal the FB.

Why upper lid eversion is critical: ~80% of foreign bodies hide under the upper lid (superior fornix and upper tarsal conjunctiva). Many patients with FBS sensation have had the FB missed because the upper lid was not everted. Never declare "no FB" without everting both lids.

Desmarres retractor: Used when lid cannot be everted by simple technique (lid oedema, scarring, blepharospasm, mass, malignancy).

❓ Viva Questions — Eye Drop Instillation
VivaWhy does only 1 drop need to be instilled? What happens to the rest?▾

Conjunctival sac capacity: The inferior fornix can hold approximately 7–10 microlitres (0.007–0.01ml) of fluid at any given time.

One standard eye drop = approximately 40–50 microlitres (0.04–0.05ml).

When the drop is instilled → the conjunctival sac immediately overflows → ~85% of the drop spills onto the cheek or drains through the NLD. Only 5–10 microlitres are retained on the ocular surface — exactly the volume the sac can hold.

Instilling 2 drops simultaneously: The second drop simply washes out the first drop before it can be absorbed — no benefit, only waste and increased systemic absorption. If a second instillation is truly needed, wait at least 5 minutes (allows first drop to absorb, refill is from tear secretion).

Practical implication: Instil exactly 1 drop per application per eye. For multiple different drops → minimum 5-minute interval between each. Ointment always last (occludes surface, reduces subsequent drop absorption).

VivaWhat is the nasolacrimal duct occlusion technique? Which drugs make it mandatory?▾

Technique: After instilling the drop and gently closing the eye, press the index finger over the inner corner of the eye (medial canthus) over the lacrimal sac for 1–2 minutes. This compresses the puncta and blocks drainage into the nasolacrimal duct.

Why: The nasolacrimal duct drains into the inferior meatus of the nasal cavity. The nasal mucosa has a rich vascular supply → topical ophthalmic drugs drained here are rapidly absorbed systemically, bypassing hepatic first-pass metabolism → high systemic bioavailability → side effects. NLD occlusion keeps drug in conjunctival sac longer → better local effect + reduced systemic exposure.

Drugs where NLD occlusion is MANDATORY:

Timolol 0.5% (beta-blocker): Systemic β2-blockade → bronchospasm (fatal in asthmatics/COPD), bradycardia, hypotension, masking of hypoglycaemia in diabetics. One of the most common causes of iatrogenic bronchospasm in ophthalmology. CI: Asthma, COPD, 2nd/3rd degree AV block, cardiac failure, Raynaud's.

Pilocarpine 2–4%: Systemic cholinergic effects → salivation, sweating, abdominal cramps, bradycardia.

Phenylephrine 10%: Alpha-1 agonist → systemic hypertension, tachycardia, risk of hypertensive crisis. Use only 2.5% in children, elderly, cardiovascular disease. (Phenylephrine 2.5% is preferred in most routine dilations.)

Atropine 1% in children: Systemic anticholinergic — fever, tachycardia, flushing, hallucinations, dry mouth. Use cyclopentolate 1% instead in children (shorter duration, less systemic toxicity).

VivaWhat is the correct order when multiple drops and ointment are prescribed together?▾

Correct order:

1. Aqueous drops first (in order of ascending viscosity and ascending pH)
2. Suspend drops next (e.g. prednisolone acetate suspension — shake well before use)
3. Gel formulations (e.g. carbomer gel, polyacrylic acid)
4. Ointment always last

Why ointment last: Ointment creates a lipophilic barrier over the ocular surface → subsequent drops cannot penetrate or absorb properly. Ointment is applied last to maintain the drug film for hours (often at bedtime for this reason).

Minimum interval between drops: 5 minutes — allows the first drop to be absorbed from the precorneal pocket, nasolacrimal drainage to normalise, and tear film to reconstitute before the next drop. Less than 5 minutes → first drop washed out by second.

Practical example (post-cataract surgery): Prednisolone 1% → Moxifloxacin 0.5% → NSAID → Ointment (bedtime only). Each separated by 5 minutes.

VivaHow do you instil eye drops in a child who won't cooperate?▾

Technique for uncooperative child:

1. Swaddling technique: Wrap child firmly in a blanket/sheet with arms inside → immobilises limbs
2. One person holds the child's head still; another or a parent holds limbs
3. Closed-eye technique: If child refuses to open eyes → instil drop in the inner canthal area (medial canthus) with lids closed. When child eventually opens eyes → drop flows into conjunctival sac by capillary action
4. Bribery/distraction: Older toddlers — toys, phone screen, asking them to blow on something (blowing → lid opening)
5. For very young infants → lie supine, fix head between carer's knees, retract lower lid gently
6. Cycloplegic refraction in clinic → parent instils drops at home 1h before appointment (child in familiar environment, less distress)

Important: Never force the eye open with excessive force (risk of corneal abrasion, scleral injury in infants). If truly impossible → refer for drops under GA/sedation for examination under anaesthesia (EUA) if clinically indicated.

❓ Viva Questions — Slit Lamp Examination
VivaWhat is the principle of slit lamp? Name the illumination techniques used.▾

Principle: The slit lamp (biomicroscope) is a binocular stereoscopic microscope combined with a high-intensity slit beam of light. The illuminating and viewing systems are co-focal and can be rotated independently to examine transparent/translucent ocular structures in optical cross-section.

Illumination techniques:

1. Diffuse illumination: Wide beam, low magnification → overview of lids, conjunctiva, corneal surface
2. Focal/Direct illumination (parallelepiped): Medium beam → 3D optical section of cornea and lens; see KPs, infiltrates, cells
3. Optic section: Very narrow slit → cross-section of cornea (see layers) or lens (nuclear vs cortical cataract)
4. Specular reflection: Angle of incidence = angle of reflection → examine corneal endothelium (mosaic pattern), Descemet's folds
5. Indirect (retro-illumination): Light reflected from deeper structure illuminates more anterior structure from behind → corneal vessels, ghost vessels, KPs, iris transillumination defects
6. Sclerotic scatter: Total internal reflection within cornea → corneal opacities glow white against dark background
7. Proximal illumination: Light beam placed adjacent to area of interest

VivaDescribe the systematic approach to examining the anterior segment at the slit lamp.▾

Systematic order (always anterior to posterior, right eye first):

1. Eyelids: Skin, lashes (trichiasis/distichiasis), lid margins (blepharitis, notching), meibomian orifices (plugging), lid position (ptosis, ectropion, entropion), eversion of upper lid for tarsal conjunctiva

2. Conjunctiva:
Palpebral: papillae (cobblestone = VKC), follicles (viral/chlamydial), membranes
Bulbar: congestion type (conjunctival vs circumcorneal), chemosis, lesions, pterygium

3. Cornea: Size, shape, surface sheen, transparency, vascularisation, KPs (location/type), epithelial defect (fluorescein staining), ulcer (size/depth/margins), scars, infiltrates, guttatae

4. Anterior chamber: Depth (van Herick grading: compare AC depth to peripheral corneal thickness; grade 1–4; grade 1 = very shallow = narrow angle risk), content — cells, flare (Tyndall effect), hypopyon, hyphema, fibrin

5. Iris: Colour, pattern, atrophy (transillumination), rubeosis, nodules, coloboma, synechiae (anterior/posterior), tremulous (phakodonesis = lens subluxation)

6. Pupil: Size, shape, reactions, synechiae

7. Lens: Clear/opaque, position (decentration, subluxation), grade (LOCS-III: NS, C, P), IOL if pseudophakic

8. Anterior vitreous: Cells (vitritis), syneresis

VivaWhat is the eclipse sign? How do you elicit it and what does it indicate?▾

Eclipse (shadow) sign: When a light is shone from the temporal side of the eye, a crescent-shaped shadow falls on the nasal part of the iris in an eye with a shallow anterior chamber.

Mechanism: In a normal (deep) AC → the iris is flat → light illuminates the entire iris uniformly from temporal side. In a shallow AC → the peripheral iris bows forward (convex iris) → the temporal nasal iris is in the shadow cast by the anteriorly displaced temporal iris → crescent shadow visible on nasal iris.

How to elicit: Shine a torch/pen light from the temporal side, parallel to the iris plane. Observe whether a shadow falls on the nasal half of the iris.

Grading (van Herick at slit lamp — more precise):
Grade 4: AC depth = corneal thickness (deep, open angle — safe)
Grade 3: AC = 1/2 corneal thickness
Grade 2: AC = 1/4 corneal thickness (narrow, at risk)
Grade 1: AC <1/4 corneal thickness (very narrow — likely closure with dilation)
Grade 0: No AC — closed

Clinical significance: Positive eclipse sign / grade 1–2 van Herick = narrow anterior chamber angle → risk of primary angle closure glaucoma (PACG). Do NOT dilate the pupil in such patients without gonioscopy to confirm → dilation can precipitate acute angle closure attack.

VivaWhat is fluorescein staining? What does it show?▾

Fluorescein sodium: A water-soluble orange dye that emits bright green fluorescence when illuminated with blue (cobalt blue) light (excitation 490nm, emission 520nm).

Properties: Stains hydrophilic (water-containing) defects in the epithelium — does NOT penetrate intact epithelium or mucus (mucus quenches fluorescence).

What it shows:
1. Corneal epithelial defects (erosions, ulcers, abrasions) — bright green staining
2. Dendritic/geographic ulcer (HSV keratitis) — branching green staining with terminal bulbs
3. Punctate epithelial erosions (dry eye, medicamentosa, exposure, UV keratitis — welder's flash)
4. Seidel test — dark streaming aqueous diluting concentrated fluorescein = perforation/wound leak
5. Tear break-up time (TBUT) — time between blink and first dry spot appearing in fluorescein-stained tear film (normal ≥10 sec; <10 sec = tear instability/dry eye)
6. Lacrimal patency (Jones test) — fluorescein recovery from nasal passage confirms NLD patency
7. Applanation tonometry — fluorescein cobalt blue light → green semicircles for IOP measurement (Goldmann tonometer)

Rose Bengal: Different dye — stains devitalised (dead/damaged) epithelial cells and mucus. Used for HSV (stains active edge of dendrite brilliantly), dry eye assessment, and conjunctival impression cytology.

Summary Sheet

Quick Revision — All Topics

🔑 Ptosis Surgery

Good LF (12–14mm) → Levator resection/plication/mullerectomy. Fair (5–11mm) → Levator resection. Poor (<4mm) → Frontalis sling. Congenital severe → operate early (amblyopia risk). Mild/moderate → wait till 3–4 yrs.

🔑 Corneal Layers

Epithelium → Bowman's → Stroma (90%) → Dua's layer → Descemet's → Endothelium. Endothelium = pump; does NOT regenerate → bullous keratopathy if damaged.

🔑 Corneal Ulcer Rules

Scrape from EDGE (organisms more at margins). KOH → Aspergillus = branched septate hyphae; Candida = pseudohyphae. SDA culture. NO steroids in any infective ulcer — EXCEPT Mooren's. Vegetative trauma → prophylactic antifungal. HSV → acyclovir, NO steroids.

🔑 Uveitis Pearls

KPs in Arlt's triangle. Mutton fat KPs = granulomatous. Koeppe nodules = pupil margin. Busacca = iris stroma. Both = granulomatous. IOP ↑ acute / ↓ chronic. Posterior synechiae → iris bombé → secondary angle closure. Treat: topical steroids + cycloplegics.

🔑 Cataract

2nd sight = nuclear cataract (index myopia). PSC = worst VA for size, caused by steroids. Steroid cataract = PSC. Snowflake = DM. Sunflower = Cu (Wilson's). Siderosis = Fe. SICS = Small Incision Cataract Surgery. PCO = most common LATE complication. Endophthalmitis = most serious early complication.

🔑 Glaucoma

Only modifiable RF = ↓ IOP. ISNT rule (normal NRR: Inferior > Superior > Nasal > Temporal). VF progression: paracentral → Seidel's → Bjerrum's → double arcuate + nasal step → tubular → temporal island. Glaucoma VF = HORIZONTAL defects. NTG = damage despite normal IOP (vascular etiology).

🔑 TED Surgery Order

DECOMPRESSION first → SQUINT surgery → EYELID surgery. This sequence is mandatory because decompression changes EOM alignment. Optic neuropathy in TED = IV methylprednisolone 500mg–1g × 3 days.

🔑 Lacrimal

DCR = operation of choice for adult NLD block. DCT when DCR contraindicated (TB, syphilis, leprosy, sac tumor, fibrotic sac). Congenital NLD block in infants → probing at 1 year if not spontaneously resolved.

🔑 Pupils

NEVER dilate in head injury / organophosphate poisoning. RAPD = afferent (optic nerve) defect. Argyll Robertson = light-near dissociation (syphilis). Hutchinson = uncal herniation. Horner's = miosis + ptosis + anhidrosis. Anisocoria > bright light = parasympathetic defect; > dim light = sympathetic defect (Horner's).

🔑 Red Eye Key Differentials

Conjunctivitis = conjunctival congestion, discharge, no pain. Keratitis/Iritis/AACG = circumcorneal congestion, pain, vision ↓. Halos + nausea + mid-dilated fixed pupil + rock-hard eye = AACG — emergency. Eclipse sign positive = shallow AC = narrow angle risk.

🔑 Pterygium

UV exposure, hot climate, limbal stem cell deficiency. Stocker's line = iron anterior to head. Probe passes under pseudopterygium, not true pterygium. Recurrence is main problem. Best surgery = excision + CLAU (conjunctival limbal autograft). MMC reduces recurrence.

Eyelid & Ptosis
EyelidDefine ptosis. How do you grade it?▾

Definition: Abnormal drooping of the upper eyelid below its normal position. Normal upper lid covers 2mm (1/5th) of the cornea. MRD1 (Margin Reflex Distance 1) = distance from upper lid margin to corneal light reflex = normally 3–4mm.

Grading by amount of ptosis: Mild = 2mm | Moderate = 2–4mm | Severe = >4mm

Levator function (excursion): Frontalis blocked with examiner's thumb; measure lid movement from full downgaze to full upgaze.
Normal ≥15mm | Good = 12–14mm | Fair = 5–11mm | Poor ≤4mm

MRD2 (lower lid to corneal reflex) = normally 5mm.

EyelidClassify ptosis. Which is most common in adults? Children? (RGUHS)▾

2. Myogenic: Myasthenia gravis (fatigable ptosis — worse evening, Cogan's twitch sign, ice-pack test positive), Myotonic dystrophy (bilateral), Ocular myopathy, Simple congenital

3. Aponeurotic: Senile involutional (most common in adults — LPS aponeurosis dehiscence), post-surgical, blepharochalasis

EyelidWhat is Bell's phenomenon? Why does it matter for ptosis surgery?▾

On attempted lid closure, the eyeball rotates upward and outward (physiological upward rotation). This protects the cornea when lids close incompletely.

Surgical significance: In ptosis surgery, if overcorrected → lagophthalmos (incomplete closure). If Bell's is good → cornea rolls up behind lid → protected. If Bell's is absent or poor (e.g. CN7 palsy, LPS fibrosis) → cornea exposed → exposure keratitis, corneal ulcer.

Always assess Bell's pre-operatively. Absent Bell's = relative contraindication to aggressive correction; plan conservative surgery.

EyelidDescribe Marcus Gunn jaw-winking syndrome▾

Congenital unilateral ptosis in which the ptotic lid elevates (winks) when the jaw moves — on chewing, mouth opening, or jaw lateral movement.

Mechanism: Aberrant innervation — synkinesis between pterygoid muscles (CN5) and levator palpebrae superioris (CN3). When pterygoids are activated → LPS fires → lid elevates.

Prevalence: ~5% of all congenital ptosis cases.

Management: Mild jaw-winking → observation. Significant cosmetic/functional → bilateral LPS disinsertion + bilateral frontalis sling (must be bilateral to symmetrise lid height at rest and on jaw movement).

EyelidWhat is Myasthenia Gravis ptosis? How do you test for it?▾

MG causes fatigable ptosis due to autoantibodies against acetylcholine receptors at NMJ. Ptosis worsens with sustained upgaze or by end of the day.

Signs: Bilateral ptosis (may be asymmetric), fatigable EOM (diplopia variable), orbicularis weakness, no pupil involvement.

Tests:

Electromyography: Repetitive nerve stimulation → decremental response.

Cornea & Corneal Ulcers
CorneaName the layers of cornea. Which is absent vs sclera? Properties of each layer? (Vydehi)▾

6 layers (anterior → posterior):

1. Epithelium — 5–6 layers non-keratinized squamous; basal cells regenerate; tight junctions (barrier); heals rapidly (24–48h for abrasion)

2. Bowman's membrane — acellular modified anterior stroma; does NOT regenerate once damaged → permanent scar; absent in sclera

3. Stroma (90%) — type I collagen in regular parallel lamellae (key for transparency); keratocytes; edema → irregular lamellae → haze

4. Dua's layer (2013 — Harminder Dua) — strong, acellular; between stroma and Descemet's; relevant for DALK (big bubble technique)

5. Descemet's membrane — basement membrane of endothelium; very elastic; absent in sclera; can herniate through perforations (Descemetocele)

6. Endothelium — single layer hexagonal cells; Na-K-ATPase pump maintains corneal dehydration; normal count 2500–3000 cells/mm²; CANNOT regenerate → endothelial failure → bullous keratopathy

Absent in sclera: Bowman's membrane and Descemet's membrane. Also sclera has irregular collagen arrangement → opaque.

CorneaWhat are the properties of the cornea that make it transparent?▾

5 factors maintaining corneal transparency:

1. Avascularity — no blood vessels to scatter light

2. Regular arrangement of stromal collagen lamellae — uniform spacing (220nm) → destructive interference of scattered light (Maurice's lattice theory) → only forward transmission

3. Relative dehydration of stroma (78% water) — maintained by endothelial pump (Na-K-ATPase) and epithelial barrier. Edema → irregular lamellae → haze

4. Smooth refracting surface — maintained by precorneal tear film (contributes 43D of total 58D corneal power)

5. No keratinization of epithelium — transparent cells

KPs (Keratic Precipitates): Clumps of inflammatory cells (lymphocytes, macrophages, epithelioid cells) deposited on posterior corneal endothelium.

CorneaWhat is DSEK/DMEK? When is it preferred over PK?▾

Lens & Cataract
LensWhat is "second sight" phenomenon? Mechanism? (Vydehi viva)▾

Second sight = A previously presbyopic patient who needed reading glasses finds they can suddenly read without glasses (sometimes even near print). They appear to have "regained" near vision.

Mechanism: In nuclear sclerotic cataract, the crystalline lens nucleus hardens and its refractive index increases → acts as a more powerful converging lens → eye shifts towards index myopia. This myopic shift coincidentally corrects presbyopia → the patient can read again without glasses.

Important clinical points:

- Distance vision simultaneously worsens (myopic shift)
- This is a transient phase as cataract continues to mature
- Second sight is a warning sign of progressive nuclear cataract
- Also seen in: uncontrolled DM (osmotic lens swelling → temporary myopia)

LensEnumerate causes of leukocoria. What must you rule out first and why?▾

Leukocoria = white pupillary reflex (cat's eye reflex). Detected on torch examination or Brückner test.

Causes (mnemonic: R-CROP-CONN):
Retinoblastoma | Coats' disease | ROP (retinopathy of prematurity) | Organised vitreous haemorrhage | PHVP/PHPV (persistent hyperplastic primary vitreous) | Congenital cataract | Optic nerve coloboma / choroidal coloboma | Norrie's disease / retinal dysplasia | Neurosensory RD

Most important to exclude: Retinoblastoma
- Most common intraocular malignancy in children (<5 years)
- Mutation in RB1 gene (chromosome 13q14) — tumour suppressor
- Bilateral (25–30%) → heritable; Unilateral (70–75%) mostly sporadic
- Investigations: B-scan USG (calcification = pathognomonic), CT/MRI orbit, LDH levels, bone marrow biopsy for staging
- NEVER do biopsy → orbital seeding, metastasis
- Treatment: Intra-arterial chemotherapy (IAC), systemic chemo, enucleation for advanced cases, external beam RT (avoid if possible → secondary tumours)

LensWhat is PCO? Pathogenesis, treatment and prevention?▾

PCO = Posterior Capsule Opacification — most common late complication of cataract surgery (up to 50% at 5 years).

Pathogenesis: Residual lens epithelial cells (LECs) from the equatorial lens bow (Sommering's ring) and anterior capsule → proliferate, migrate onto posterior capsule → Elschnig pearls (bladder cell type, more common) or fibrous metaplasia → visual axis opacification.

Symptoms: Gradual progressive blurring of vision months–years after successful cataract surgery, glare, reduced contrast sensitivity.

Treatment: Nd:YAG laser posterior capsulotomy — 1–2mJ pulses create central opening in posterior capsule. Quick outpatient procedure. Complications: IOP spike, IOL pitting, CME, RD (rare).

Prevention: Hydrophobic acrylic IOL with sharp posterior optic edge (barrier against LEC migration), complete removal of anterior epithelial cells during surgery, in-the-bag fixation, square-edge IOL design, IOL materials that reduce LEC adhesion.

LensClassify cataracts by morphology. Name drug-induced cataracts. (RGUHS short answer)▾

Morphological classification (LOCS-III system):
Nuclear (NS1–NS5): Yellow→amber→brown; 2nd sight phenomenon; best vision with dilated pupil or dim light
Cortical (C1–C5): Spoke-like opacities from periphery; worse in bright light (pupil constricts → light through opaque cortex)
PSC (P1–P5): Posterior subcapsular; worst VA for smallest opacity (bright light → miosis → image through PSC); worst for reading, driving at night; caused by steroids/radiation/DM/RP

Drug-induced cataracts:
- Corticosteroids → PSC (dose and duration dependent)
- Chlorpromazine (phenothiazines) → anterior capsular star-shaped opacity
- Amiodarone → anterior subcapsular
- Miotics (long-acting anticholinesterases: echothiophate) → anterior subcapsular
- Busulphan, statins (controversial)

Other cataracts to know: DM → snowflake/true diabetic (young DM); Wilson's → sunflower (copper); Siderosis (iron FB) → brown staining; Chalcosis (copper) → sunflower; Hypocalcemia → fine subcapsular punctate; Galactosemia → oil droplet (infant); Marfan's → ectopia lentis (up and out)

Glaucoma
GlaucomaDefine and classify glaucoma (RGUHS 10-mark question)▾

Definition: Chronic progressive optic neuropathy characterised by specific structural optic disc changes (CDR increase, ISNT rule violation, disc haemorrhage) and corresponding visual field changes, in which lowering of IOP is the only proven modifiable risk factor. IOP normal = 10–21 mmHg.

Classification:

A. Primary Glaucoma (no identifiable secondary cause):
1. POAG (Primary Open Angle Glaucoma) — most common; insidious; bilateral; no pain
2. PACG (Primary Angle Closure Glaucoma) — Asian predilection; angle closure by iris-lens contact
3. NTG (Normal Tension Glaucoma) — damage despite IOP <21mmHg; vascular etiology
4. OHT (Ocular Hypertension) — raised IOP without disc/VF changes; conversion risk ~1% per year
5. Primary Congenital Glaucoma (PCG) — buphthalmos in infants

B. Secondary Glaucoma:
Open angle: Steroid-induced, uveitic, pigment dispersion, pseudoexfoliation, phacolytic (hypermature cataract), phacoanaphylactic
Closed angle: Rubeotic (NV from DM/CRAO), phacomorphic (intumescent lens), plateau iris, malignant glaucoma (aqueous misdirection)

C. Developmental: PCG, juvenile OAG, associated with developmental anomalies (Axenfeld-Rieger, Peters')

GlaucomaDescribe VF changes in glaucoma in order of progression. What is the ISNT rule?▾

Visual field defects — order of progression:
1. Superior paracentral scotoma (earliest — corresponds to inferior NRR thinning)
2. Seidel's scotoma — paracentral joins blind spot (Mariotte's)
3. Bjerrum's (arcuate) scotoma — arcuate from blind spot around fixation to nasal horizontal raphe
4. Double arcuate scotoma (superior + inferior) + Roenne's nasal step (where arcuates meet at horizontal raphe — different sensitivities above and below)
5. Tubular vision — only central 5° remains
6. Temporal island of vision — only far peripheral temporal vision
7. Total blindness

Important: Glaucoma VF defects = HORIZONTAL (respecting horizontal midline = nasal raphe). Neurological VF defects = VERTICAL (respecting vertical midline at fovea).

ISNT rule: Normal NRR (Neuroretinal Rim) thickness: Inferior > Superior > Nasal > Temporal. In glaucoma, the ISNT rule is violated — thinning begins inferotemporal first.

Paracentral scotoma: Within 10° of fixation. Arcuate (Bjerrum's) scotoma: 10–20° from fixation in arcuate pattern following NFL bundles.

GlaucomaHow does corneal thickness affect IOP measurement? (Singi Yatra)▾

Goldmann Applanation Tonometry (GAT) is calibrated assuming central corneal thickness (CCT) of 520–530μm.

Thick cornea (>530μm): Overestimates IOP. Measured IOP is falsely elevated → patient may be labelled OHT when IOP is actually normal. Correct by subtracting approximately 0.7mmHg for every 10μm above 530μm.

Thin cornea (<520μm): Underestimates IOP. Falsely low readings → IOP may appear normal while actually elevated. More dangerous clinically. Also an independent risk factor for glaucoma progression (thinner cornea = structural vulnerability). Correct by adding 0.7mmHg for every 10μm below 520μm.

Clinical significance: Post-LASIK/PRK patients have thin corneas (from ablation) → measured IOP always underestimated → must use non-contact methods (Ocular Response Analyzer, Dynamic Contour Tonometer) or apply correction. Also important in keratoconus (very thin corneas).

GlaucomaWhat are anti-glaucoma drugs? Classify with mechanism of action.▾

Classification:

1. Reduce aqueous production:
Beta-blockers (Timolol 0.5%, Betaxolol, Carteolol) — inhibit ciliary body β2 receptors → ↓ aqueous secretion. CI: Asthma, bradycardia, COPD. NLD occlusion mandatory (systemic absorption → bradycardia/bronchospasm)
CAIs (Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors) — Acetazolamide (oral/IV), Dorzolamide, Brinzolamide (topical) — block CA-II in ciliary body → ↓ HCO₃ → ↓ aqueous. SE: Metabolic acidosis, renal stones (oral form)
Alpha-2 agonists (Brimonidine 0.2%) — dual: ↓ production + ↑ uveoscleral outflow

2. Increase aqueous outflow:
Prostaglandin analogues (Latanoprost, Bimatoprost, Travoprost) — ↑ uveoscleral outflow via FP-receptor activation. Most potent IOP-lowering agents (25–35% reduction). Once daily, evening. SE: Hyperemia, hypertrichosis, iris/periorbital pigmentation, CME (aphakic/pseudophakic — caution)
Miotics (Pilocarpine 1–4%) — M3 agonist → ciliary muscle contraction → trabecular meshwork opens → ↑ conventional outflow. SE: Miosis (↓ night vision), accommodative spasm, RD risk

3. Combined/Others: Rho-kinase inhibitors (Netarsudil) — new; fixed combinations (Cosopt = Dorzolamide + Timolol)

Uveitis
UveitisDescribe acute anterior uveitis — full clinical picture and management (RGUHS 10 marks)▾

Symptoms: Dull aching pain (worse at night; ciliary spasm), photophobia (inflamed iris contracts against light), blepharospasm, lacrimation (reflex), circumcorneal redness, defective vision. No discharge (key differentiator from conjunctivitis).

Signs (anterior to posterior):
Cornea: KPs (Arlt's triangle) — type indicates granulomatous vs non-gran
AC: Cells (inflammatory) + flare (protein — Tyndall effect) + hypopyon (sterile layered pus) + fibrinous exudate
Iris: Muddy iris (edema), miosis (ciliary spasm), posterior synechiae (PS), iris nodules (Koeppe/Busacca)
IOP: ↑ in acute (TM blocked by cells/protein) / ↓ in chronic (ciliary body suppression)
Pupil: Irregular, small; festooned (multiple PS) → fixed (annular PS) → iris bombé (360° PS → aqueous cannot pass → iris bows forward) → 2° angle closure

Complications: 2° glaucoma, band keratopathy (hypercalcaemia/chronic), complicated cataract, CME, RD, phthisis bulbi

Management:
Topical steroids: Prednisolone acetate 1% — q1h initially; taper slowly over weeks/months (never stop abruptly)
Cycloplegia: Atropine 1% (or cyclopentolate/homatropine) — relieves ciliary spasm (pain ↓), prevents and breaks PS, maintains blood-aqueous barrier
Systemic: Oral prednisolone (bilateral/severe); DMARDs for chronic/recurrent (methotrexate, azathioprine, mycophenolate)
IOP management: Beta-blockers or CAI if elevated (avoid miotics and prostaglandins in uveitic glaucoma)
Treat underlying cause: TB (ATT), sarcoid, HLA-B27 diseases

UveitisWhat is iris bombé? Explain the pathogenesis of 2° glaucoma in uveitis.▾

Iris bombé: Forward bowing of the iris caused by 360° posterior synechiae (annular synechiae) blocking the pupil completely (occlusio pupillae). Aqueous produced in posterior chamber cannot flow through pupil to anterior chamber → accumulates → pushes iris forward → iris bombé → peripheral anterior synechiae (angle closure) → 2° glaucoma.

Pathogenesis of 2° glaucoma in uveitis:

Acute (↑ IOP): Inflammatory cells and protein block trabecular meshwork → ↓ outflow → ↑ IOP

Pupil block (↑ IOP): PS → iris bombé → secondary angle closure (as above)

Peripheral anterior synechiae (PAS): Chronic inflammation → PAS → angle progressively blocked → OAG pattern with elevated IOP

Chronic (↓ IOP): Cyclitic membrane → ciliary body detachment/suppression → ↓ aqueous production → hypotony → phthisis bulbi

Management of iris bombé: Urgent Nd:YAG or surgical PI (peripheral iridotomy) to restore aqueous flow; intensive steroids + cycloplegia.

Orbit & Thyroid Eye Disease
OrbitDescribe TED — pathophysiology, clinical features, Werner's classification, management (RGUHS 10 marks)▾

Pathophysiology: Autoimmune disease. T cells sensitised against TSH receptor (shared antigen between thyroid follicular cells and orbital fibroblasts). T cells infiltrate orbit → fibroblast activation → adipogenesis + GAG synthesis (hyaluronate) + fibroblast proliferation → EOM enlargement (belly, tendon spared) + orbital fat expansion → raised intraorbital pressure → proptosis.

Clinical features — 5 components:
1. Soft tissue: grittiness, lacrimation, chemosis, periorbital edema, conjunctival congestion (Goldzieher's sign — temporal congestion)
2. Lid retraction: staring appearance, Dalrymple's, Von Graefe's, Stellwag's, Griffith's, Boston's, Rosenbach's, Means', Kocher's, Tello's, Jellinek's
3. Proptosis: axial (bilateral); measured by Hertel's; Naffziger's + worm's eye view
4. Restrictive myopathy: IR most common (↓ elevation) → diplopia; then MR → SO
5. Optic neuropathy (sight-threatening): ↓VA, ↓colour vision, RAPD, VEP delay — due to compression at orbital apex

Werner's NO SPECS: N=no signs, O=only signs, S=soft tissue, P=proptosis ≥20mm, E=EOM, C=corneal, S=sight loss (optic nerve)

Management:
Medical: Lubricants, head elevation, goggles; oral prednisolone 60–100mg/day (active disease); IV methylprednisolone 500mg–1g × 3 days × 2 weeks for optic neuropathy; selenium (mild active disease); orbital radiotherapy (20Gy)
Surgical (MANDATORY SEQUENCE): 1. Orbital decompression → 2. Squint surgery → 3. Eyelid surgery
Indication for urgent decompression: Dysthyroid optic neuropathy (DON), severe exposure keratitis, cosmetically unacceptable proptosis

OrbitEnumerate causes of proptosis and describe examination of a proptotic patient▾

Proptosis: Globe >22mm from lateral orbital rim OR >2mm asymmetry.

Causes by category:
Inflammatory: TED (#1 adult), orbital pseudotumor, orbital cellulitis (preseptal / postseptal), dacryoadenitis, Tolosa-Hunt
Vascular: Carotid-cavernous fistula (pulsatile, chemosis, bruit, high IOP), orbital varix (intermittent — Valsalva manoeuvre increases), cavernous hemangioma (#1 benign adult orbital tumour)
Neoplastic — Children: Rhabdomyosarcoma (#1 primary malignant), dermoid cyst (#1 benign), capillary hemangioma, optic nerve glioma (NF-1), metastatic neuroblastoma
Neoplastic — Adults: Lacrimal gland tumours (pleomorphic adenoma benign; adenoid cystic CA malignant), lymphoma, optic nerve meningioma, metastatic (breast, lung, prostate)
Metabolic/Osseous: Fibrous dysplasia, Paget's
Pseudoproptosis: High myopia, buphthalmos, lid retraction, contralateral ptosis, enophthalmos other side

1. General: thyroid status (goiter, tremor, tachycardia)
2. Inspection: degree (Naffziger + worm's eye view), direction (axial = intraconal; eccentric = extraconal mass), pulsatile?
3. Measure: Hertel's exophthalmometer (normal <20mm, asymmetry <2mm)
4. Assess: lid position, blink, lagophthalmos, lid signs

Lacrimal System
LacrimalDescribe DCR — indications, steps, and complications (RGUHS + Vydehi)▾

DCR (Dacryocystorhinostomy): Creates a surgical bypass between lacrimal sac and nasal mucosa through a window made in lacrimal bone → new drainage pathway.

Indications (NLD obstruction): Primary acquired NLD obstruction (most common), chronic dacryocystitis (after control of infection), failed probing in children (>1 year), trauma to NLD, dacryocystitis with mucocele.

Contraindications → DCT instead: Active TB/syphilis/leprosy of lacrimal sac, lacrimal sac malignancy, fibrotic/shrunken sac, atrophic rhinitis.

External DCR Steps:
1. GA or LA (lignocaine with adrenaline)
2. Nasal packing (xylometazoline + adrenaline pack)
3. Skin incision 10mm medial to medial canthus (straight/curvilinear)
4. Identify + protect medial palpebral ligament
5. Expose lacrimal fossa + periosteum → retract sac laterally
6. Remove lacrimal bone → create 15×15mm ostium into nasal cavity
7. Lacrimal sac marsupialisation → H-flap (anterior + posterior flaps)
8. Nasal mucosal H-flaps to match
9. Silicone bicanalicular intubation (stents — removed at 6 weeks)
10. Suture anterior flaps (key step); posterior flaps optional
11. Skin closure

Complications:
Early: Bleeding (anterior ethmoidal artery), CSF leak, orbital entry, anesthesia
Late: Re-stenosis (most common — fibrosis of ostium), granuloma formation, medial canthal scarring, dacryocystitis recurrence

Success rate: External DCR ~90–95%; Endonasal ~85–90%.

Endonasal vs External DCR advantages: No scar, shorter surgery, preserves pump function, can do bilateral simultaneously. Disadvantage: indirect view, higher skill required, slightly lower success rate.

LacrimalWhat are the tests for epiphora? How do you diagnose the level of obstruction?▾

Epiphora = excess tearing due to overproduction or impaired drainage.

A. Functional tests (confirm epiphora and assess drainage):
Fluorescein dye disappearance test (FDDT): Instil fluorescein in both conjunctival sacs → examine with cobalt blue light at 5 min → dye should clear; retention = impaired drainage (sensitive for functional block)
Jones dye test (primary): Fluorescein instilled → cotton applicator in inferior meatus at 5 min → dye present = patent, functional system. Dye absent = obstruction or pump failure
Jones dye test (secondary): After failed primary → syringe saline into punctum → if dye-stained fluid from nose = functional block (anatomy patent but pump fails); clear fluid = partial anatomical block; no fluid = complete anatomical block

B. Anatomical tests:
Syringing: Irrigate through punctum with saline → regurgitation from same punctum = canalicular block; regurgitation from opposite punctum = common canaliculus/sac block; flows into nose = patent NLD
Probing: Bowman probe passed through canaliculus to identify level of obstruction; hard stop = lacrimal bone (normal), soft stop = mucosal fold
Dacryocystography (DCG): Radio-opaque dye injected → X-ray/CT → shows exact site and nature of obstruction
Nasal endoscopy: Assess nasal pathology (septal deviation, inferior turbinate hypertrophy, polyps) that may obstruct lower NLD

Retina (High-Yield)
RetinaWhat is cherry red spot? Causes? (RGUHS short answer)▾

Cherry red spot: A bright red spot seen at the fovea against a pale, oedematous retina.

Mechanism: Retinal edema (milky white opacity) develops around the fovea due to ischaemia or lipid accumulation. The fovea appears red because it is the thinnest part of the retina (no inner layers, no oedema) → underlying choroid is visible as red/orange. Surrounding pale edema creates the contrast → cherry red spot.

Causes:
1. CRAO (Central Retinal Artery Occlusion) — most important; sudden painless severe vision loss; inner retina oedema; cherry red spot within hours → pale disc, attenuated vessels
2. Neimann-Pick disease (sphingomyelin accumulation in ganglion cells)
3. Tay-Sachs disease (GM2 gangliosidosis — hexosaminidase A deficiency)
4. Sandhoff disease, GM1 gangliosidosis, Farber disease
5. Quinine toxicity
6. Commotio retinae (traumatic — Berlin's oedema)

CRAO treatment (within 90 min is ideal): Ocular massage, anterior chamber paracentesis (reduce IOP acutely → emboli dislodges), IV acetazolamide, 100% O2 inhalation, IOP-lowering drops, rebreathing bag (5% CO2 → vasodilation), systemic workup for embolic source (carotid Doppler, Echo).

RetinaWhat is retinoblastoma? How is it diagnosed and managed?▾

Retinoblastoma: Most common intraocular malignancy in children; arises from primitive retinal cells. Mean age at diagnosis: 18 months (bilateral heritable) to 24 months (unilateral).

Genetics: RB1 gene mutation (chromosome 13q14, tumour suppressor). Knudson's two-hit hypothesis: germinal (heritable bilateral) vs somatic (non-heritable unilateral). Autosomal dominant inheritance when heritable. Survivors of heritable RB at risk of osteosarcoma and other second malignancies.

Presentation: Leukocoria (60%), strabismus (20%), red painful eye (secondary glaucoma), proptosis (advanced extraocular)

Diagnosis:
- B-scan USG: Calcification within tumour (pathognomonic — present in 95%)
- MRI orbit and brain: Extent, optic nerve involvement, trilateral RB (associated pinealoblastoma)
- NEVER biopsy — risk of tumour seeding and orbital dissemination
- LDH, urinary VMA, bone marrow biopsy for staging

Management (by IIRC / Reese-Ellsworth staging):
Small tumours: Laser photocoagulation, cryotherapy, plaque brachytherapy
Intra-arterial chemotherapy (IAC): Melphalan via ophthalmic artery → tumour regression (avoid systemic side effects)
Systemic chemotherapy: Vincristine + etoposide + carboplatin (VEC) for bilateral/advanced
Enucleation: Advanced unilateral (Reese-Ellsworth V) with optic nerve clearance of ≥10mm
External beam RT: Avoided now (second tumour risk in germinal cases)

RetinaWhat is diabetic retinopathy? Classify it and describe management. (RGUHS)▾

Diabetic retinopathy (DR): Microangiopathy of retinal capillaries due to chronic hyperglycaemia → most common cause of blindness in working-age population in developed world.

Pathogenesis: Pericyte loss → microaneurysms → vascular leakage (hard exudates, macular oedema) + vascular occlusion (cotton wool spots, ischaemia) → retinal hypoxia → VEGF release → neovascularisation.

Classification (ETDRS / International):
NPDR (Non-Proliferative):
Mild — microaneurysms only
Moderate — microaneurysms + dot-blot haemorrhages + hard exudates + CWS
Severe (pre-proliferative) — 4-2-1 rule: haemorrhages in 4 quadrants OR venous beading in 2 quadrants OR IRMA in 1 quadrant
PDR (Proliferative):
NVD (neovascularisation of disc) or NVE (elsewhere) → vitreous haemorrhage → tractional RD
CSME (Clinically Significant Macular Oedema): retinal thickening ≤500μm from foveal centre, or hard exudates ≤500μm, or ≥1 disc area oedema within 1 disc diameter of centre

Management:
Systemic: Tight glycaemic control (HbA1c <7%), BP control, lipid control, smoking cessation
Macular oedema: Intravitreal anti-VEGF (Ranibizumab/Bevacizumab/Aflibercept) — first line; focal/grid laser photocoagulation; intravitreal steroids (triamcinolone/dexamethasone implant)
PDR: PRP (Pan-Retinal Photocoagulation) — 1200–1600 burns; intravitreal anti-VEGF pre-op; vitrectomy for VH/TRD

Practical Skills — Viva
SkillsWhy is Snellen's chart placed at 6 metres? What does 6/6 mean?▾

6 metres = optical infinity: At 6m, light rays from an object are virtually parallel (divergence is negligible). The eye therefore needs minimal or no accommodation → results represent the eye's distance refractive status without accommodation influencing the result.

6/6 notation: Numerator = distance at which patient was tested (6m). Denominator = distance at which a normal eye can read that particular letter. 6/6 means the patient reads at 6m what a normal eye reads at 6m. 6/60 means the patient reads at 6m what a normal eye can read from 60m.

Below 6/60: Move patient closer until they read the top letter → notation e.g. 3/60, 2/60, 1/60. Below 1/60: CF (count fingers at Xm), HM (hand motion), PL+PR (perceives light, correct projection), PL-PR (poor projection), NPL (no perception of light).

Pinhole test: Eliminates refractive error by selecting only central paraxial rays → improvement with pinhole = refractive error; no improvement = media opacity / retinal / optic nerve disease.

SkillsWhat are the different types of pupil reflexes? Describe the pathway of the light reflex.▾

Types of pupil reactions:
1. Direct light reflex — pupil constricts when light shone in same eye
2. Consensual (indirect) light reflex — pupil constricts when light shone in other eye
3. Near (accommodation-convergence) reflex — miosis + accommodation + convergence on near fixation
4. Psychosensory reflex — pupil dilates with sudden fright/sensory stimulus
5. Orbicularis reflex — pupil constricts on sudden attempt to close lids forcefully

Light reflex pathway:
Afferent: Retinal ganglion cells → optic nerve → optic chiasma (nasal fibres cross) → optic tract → NOT LGN → pretectal nucleus (dorsal midbrain) — bilateral projection
Efferent: Pretectal nucleus → bilateral Edinger-Westphal (EW) nuclei → preganglionic parasympathetic fibres in CN3 → ciliary ganglion → short ciliary nerves → sphincter pupillae → miosis

Key clinical points:
- RAPD = afferent defect (optic nerve/retina lesion)
- CN3 palsy = efferent defect (dilated fixed pupil)
- Light-near dissociation (Argyll Robertson) = pretectal nucleus lesion (light path blocked, near path intact)
- Pupil involvement in CN3 = compressive (aneurysm); pupil spared = medical (DM, HTN)

SkillsHow do you approach a patient with corneal foreign body? What is rust ring? (Vydehi skill)▾

Assessment: History (nature of FB — metal? organic? high-velocity? time elapsed), visual acuity, document location/depth on slit lamp, Seidel test (fluorescein — streaming = perforation).

Procedure:
1. Check VA (medicolegal baseline)
2. Instil topical anaesthetic (0.5% proparacaine or 4% xylocaine)
3. Slit lamp examination — confirm superficial vs deep
4. 26G needle or FB spud — approach tangentially from side (never perpendicular → deep push)
5. Remove from edge to centre
6. Flourescein to check residual epithelial defect

Rust ring: Within 24–48 hours of metallic (iron) FB on cornea → iron oxidises → iron oxide (rust) deposits in adjacent stromal and epithelial cells → brownish ring around the FB site. Rust causes ongoing toxicity to keratocytes → must be removed.

Rust ring removal: Ideally delay 24–48h (softens and easier to remove) → Alger brush (rotary burr drill) at slit lamp under topical anaesthesia. If Alger brush unavailable → 26G needle with gentle scraping.

Post-removal: Topical antibiotic (ciprofloxacin q6h), cycloplegic (atropine 1%) if significant defect, consider patching (controversial), review in 24h. Prophylactic antifungal if vegetative material involved.

SkillsWhat is Ishihara chart? What does it test? Limitations? (Vydehi)▾

Ishihara chart: 38-plate pseudo-isochromatic colour vision test consisting of coloured dot plates in which a numeral is embedded that normal trichromats can identify but colour-deficient individuals cannot.

Tests: Red-green colour deficiency only — does NOT test blue-yellow (tritanopia). Includes: screening plates (plates 1–21 of 38, or 1–25 of 38), transformation plates (colour-deficient see different number), diagnostic plates (differentiate protan from deutan), vanishing plates.

Technique: Diffuse natural/D65 illuminant (not tungsten/LED), 75cm from eye, 3–5 sec per plate, one eye at a time, book held perpendicular to line of sight.

Scoring: Normal = ≥17/21 plates correct. <13/21 = definite colour deficiency.

Limitations:
- Only detects red-green deficiency (not blue-yellow tritanopia)
- Screening test only — not quantitative (use Farnsworth D-15 or 100-hue for severity)
- Poor illumination → false positives
- Literacy required (number recognition)
- Malingering possible
- Cannot differentiate acquired from congenital in isolation

Inheritance: Red-green colour blindness = X-linked recessive (males 8%, females 0.5%). Tritanopia = autosomal dominant (rare).

SkillsHow do you instil eye drops? Why press the NLD? What is the danger with timolol? (Vydehi practical)▾

Technique: Wash hands → verify drug/eye/concentration → tilt head back → pull lower lid down → ask patient to look up → hold dropper 1–2cm above eye (never touch ocular surface) → instil 1 drop in inferior fornix → patient gently closes eyes → NLD occlusion (press inner corner) for 1–2 minutes → wait 5 minutes between different drops → ointment always last.

Why NLD occlusion? The nasolacrimal duct drains into the inferior meatus of nose → directly into nasal mucosa with rich vascular supply → rapid systemic absorption bypassing hepatic first-pass metabolism. NLD occlusion prevents drainage → drug stays in conjunctival sac longer → better local effect + reduced systemic side effects.

Drugs where NLD occlusion is critical:

Timolol (beta-blocker): Systemic absorption → beta-2 blockade → bronchospasm (dangerous in asthmatics/COPD), bradycardia, hypotension. Contraindicated in asthma, COPD, 2nd/3rd degree AV block, bradycardia.

Pilocarpine: Systemic absorption → cholinergic effects → salivation, sweating, abdominal cramps.

Phenylephrine (10%): Systemic absorption → hypertensive crisis (especially in elderly, HTN patients, pheochromocytoma). Use 2.5% in children/elderly.

Atropine (children): Systemic anticholinergic toxicity (fever, tachycardia, flushing, psychosis) → use cyclopentolate 1% instead in children.
